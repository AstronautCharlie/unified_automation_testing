  ---
  Categorization Method & Physical Reasoning

  Three sensors drive the classification, applied as a hierarchy:

  Step 1 — Detect lying via the BACK IMU accelerometer

  Physical insight: An accelerometer measures the sum of gravity and dynamic
  acceleration projected onto each of its body-frame axes. When a person stands,
   sits, or walks, their back is roughly vertical. In this dataset the BACK
  IMU's X-axis is aligned with "up" when the person is upright, so BACK_accX ≈ 
  −1000 (milli-g) while accY and accZ are near zero. When the person lies down,
  the back rotates ~90° and gravity shifts from the X-axis onto the Z-axis (accZ
   → +886, as confirmed in the data, while accX → near 0).

  Feature: back_vert_ratio = BACK_accX / |BACK_acc_total|. Upright → near −1.0.
  Lying → near 0. Smoothed with a 1.5-second rolling median (~45 samples at 30
  Hz) to filter transient motion.

  Rule: If back_vert_ratio > −0.40 → Lie

  ---
  Step 2 — Detect walking via SHOE IMU angular velocity

  Physical insight: During each footstep, the foot swings forward and backward
  (the gait swing phase), rotating rapidly around the ankle and hip joints. This
   produces spikes in the shoe-mounted IMU's angular velocity: each footstep is
  a burst of high angular velocity followed by near-zero (stance phase). When
  standing or sitting, the feet barely move → angular velocity is almost zero
  and very consistent. The key discriminator is therefore the variance of foot
  angular velocity, not its mean.

  Feature: Magnitude of both shoe gyros averaged together: |ω| = sqrt(ωx² + ωy² 
  + ωz²), then rolling standard deviation over 1.5 seconds.

  Rule: If shoe_angvel_std > 800 (raw IMU units) → Walk

  ---
  Step 3 — Distinguish sitting from standing via the knee accelerometer (RKN^)

  Physical insight: The RKN^ sensor is on the right knee (upper leg side). This
  is the most subtle distinction, because both postures have an upright back and
   stationary feet. The key is knee angle:

  - Standing: The thigh is vertical. Gravity runs along the thigh's long axis,
  but in this sensor's mounting, that projects minimally onto the Z-axis →
  RKN^_accZ ≈ 50–300.
  - Sitting: The thigh rotates to horizontal (~90° bend at the hip). This
  rotation brings gravity directly into the sensor's Z-axis plane → RKN^_accZ ≈ 
  600–1000.

  The data confirms a clear bimodal split in RKN^_accZ during upright,
  stationary periods: ~35% of samples fall below 200, ~30% above 600 — exactly
  consistent with two distinct thigh orientations.

  Rule: If knee_accZ > 500 → Sit, otherwise → Stand

  ---
  Decision Hierarchy

  1.  back_vert_ratio > -0.40?   →  Lie
  2.  shoe_angvel_std  > 800?    →  Walk
  3.  knee_accZ        > 500?    →  Sit
  4.  (default)                  →  Stand

  The hierarchy is physically principled: lying produces the largest whole-body
  orientation change (unambiguous), walking produces distinctive rhythmic foot
  motion, and the knee angle disambiguates the two stationary upright postures.
