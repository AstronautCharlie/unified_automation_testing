❯ In the `dataset` folder, a parquet file called `ADL_no_label.parquet`.    
  That file contains timeseries data of sensor readings taken while an      
  individual performs a series of activities of daily living (ADL). The     
  `MILLISEC` column indicates the time at which the sensor readings were    
  taken. The `recording` column has values of the format SX-ADLY, where X   
  and Y are numbers. The X value indicates the individual, and the Y value  
  indicates the run number of the activities (each individual performs a    
  number of runs of the same activities.                                    
                                                                            
  Some of the sensor data (columns 2 to 134) comes from sensors that the    
  individual wore on their body. The names of the columns indicate where on 
   the body the sensor was worn. Abbreviations are used in the column names 
   as follows: "RKN" means "right knee", "LUA" means "left upper arm", "LH" 
   means "left hand", "RUA" means "right upper arm", "RWR" means "right     
  wrist", "RUA" means "right upper arm", "LWR" means "left wrist", "RH"     
  means "right hand". Note that some body parts have more than one sensor,  
  differentiated by symbols ("^" and "_") in the column names. When this    
  happens, that is because the two sensors are placed close together.       
                                                                            
  Some of the sensor data (columns 135-231) comes from sensors attached to  
  objects. The name of the object to which the sensor is attached appears   
  in the column name. Most of these sensors are accelerometers tracking     
  acceleration in 3-dimensional space, but some are reed switches. The reed 
   switches can report values either 0 or 1, and are intended to            
  distinguish between three states (closed, half open, and fully open) for  
  the object to which they are attached.                                    
                                                                            
  Some of the sensor data (columns 232 to 243) are location tags, which I   
  believe indicate where in the space the individual is, but I'm not sure   
  as the documentation on these fields is lacking.

  ---
  Physical reasoning

  1. Walk — foot angular velocity

  The IMUs on both shoes measure angular velocity in the shoe's body frame.
  During walking, each foot swings forward in a pendulum arc and then plants on 
  the ground, repeating ~1–2 times per second. This generates sustained, large
  angular velocities (typically 2,000–8,000 in dataset units). During standing,
  sitting, or lying, the feet are stationary → near-zero angular velocity.

  I compute the magnitude of angular velocity from both shoes, take the maximum
  (either foot could be stepping), apply a 3-second rolling mean to capture
  sustained motion (not just a one-frame spike from a fidget), and threshold at
  2,000.

  2. Lie — back inclination from vertical

  The BACK IMU measures the orientation of the torso. At rest, its acceleration
  is just gravity. Empirically, the sensor's X-axis is aligned with the upright 
  body axis (mean ≈ −862 milli-g, i.e., −0.86g, when standing). I compute the
  tilt angle: arccos(−accX / |acc|). When standing or sitting, this is small
  (~10–25°). When lying down, the entire torso rotates ~90°, so this tilt
  exceeds 60°.

  I also require that foot motion is minimal (shoe angular velocity < 500) to
  avoid confusing bending forward during standing or walking — which also tilts
  the back — with lying.

  3. Sit vs. Stand — thigh orientation from knee sensor

  Both sitting and standing have feet flat on the floor and back roughly
  upright, so foot and back sensors alone can't distinguish them. The key
  difference is knee angle:

  - Standing: knee is straight, so the thigh is vertical. The knee
  accelerometer's Y-axis — aligned along the length of the thigh — carries most
  of the gravity signal (~900–1,000 milli-g).
  - Sitting: the hip and knee flex to ~90°, rotating the thigh to roughly
  horizontal. The Y-axis of the sensor now points sideways instead of downward,
  so its gravity component drops to ~200–500 milli-g.

  The data shows a clear bimodal distribution in the stationary+upright subset:
  ~40% of samples below 650 milli-g (sitting) and ~60% above (standing). The 650
   threshold sits cleanly in the trough between modes.

  4. Temporal smoothing

  After point-wise classification, I apply:
  1. A 3-second majority-vote filter to smooth label sequences (removes isolated
   misclassified frames)
  2. A minimum Lie duration of 5 seconds — a person lying down stays there for
  at least that long; shorter detections are physical impossibilities (back
  briefly tilting during a reach or bend) and are reverted to Stand
